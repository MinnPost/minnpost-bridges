While most of us only give thought to the condition of bridges when something catastrophic happens, engineers at MnDOT monitor bridges’ conditions continuously, hoping to avoid just the sorts of disasters that lead to headlines.

The safety and suitability of bridges depends on a number of factors: roadway condition and geometry, amount of traffic and structural condition, to name a few. Looking at structural condition alone, MnDOT can put the state’s more then 3,000 bridges into 4 general categories: Good, Satisfactory, Fair and Poor. These general ratings are actually a summary of a number of different aspects affecting a bridge’s structural condition: the state of its deck, the structure above and below the bridge, the condition of the culvert that carries water under a roadway, an overall structural evaluation and the adequacy of the waterway crossed by a bridge. Details of how the bridges are classified can be found in MnDOT’s Bridge Performance Measures Summary [PDF].

In general, Minnesota’s bridges are currently in good shape, with structural conditions inside MnDOT’s targets. 86% of bridges in the state are in Good or Satisfactory condition, while 12% are Fair and 2% Poor. Good and Satisfactory bridges are generally candidates for preventive maintenance — things like sealing cracks and other minor repairs — which is funded out of MnDOT’s operating budget. Fair and Poor bridges are more likely to need more significant repairs funded by capital investments, funded by federal and state road construction dollars.

MnDOT projects sufficient funding to continue to meet structural goals for all the state’s bridges for the next ten years. Beyond that, however, the agency sees expenses exceeding revenues and, absent new funding, will need to make choices about which bridge repairs to prioritize.

Of particular concern is the coming wave of maintenance needed for bridges built during the 1960s Interstate build-out. The 1960s saw the greatest number of bridges constructed of any decade so far — 857 bridges were constructed in those years, 22% of all bridges in the state. As these bridges age, their maintenance requirements are set to increase.
